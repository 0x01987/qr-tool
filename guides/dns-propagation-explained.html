DNS Propagation Explained

DNS propagation is the delay between changing a DNS record and seeing that change everywhere on the internet. It is not usually instant because DNS answers get cached by browsers, devices, internet providers, and DNS resolvers.

Simple explanation

When you update a DNS record — like pointing your domain to a new server or adding an email TXT record — not every system checks the change immediately. Many systems keep an older answer in memory for a while. That waiting period is what people call DNS propagation.

Important: In many cases, the new record is already published — the internet is just still catching up.

Why propagation happens

DNS caches answers

DNS systems cache results so they do not have to ask the authoritative server every single time. This makes the internet faster, but it also means changes can take time to appear.

TTL controls cache lifetime

TTL stands for Time To Live. It tells resolvers how long they can keep an answer before checking again.

What TTL means

TTL is usually measured in seconds. For example:

  • 300 = 5 minutes
  • 3600 = 1 hour
  • 86400 = 24 hours

If a resolver cached your old record with a TTL of 3600 seconds, it may keep showing that older value until the cache expires.

Where DNS can be cached

  • Your browser
  • Your computer or phone
  • Your router
  • Your ISP’s DNS servers
  • Public DNS resolvers like Google or Cloudflare

What propagation looks like in real life

After you change DNS, one person may see the new website while another person still sees the old one. One email provider may validate your TXT record right away while another still cannot find it. This does not always mean the change failed — it often means caches have not expired yet.

  1. Update the record in your DNS provider.
  2. Wait for the provider to publish the change.
  3. Resolvers around the internet gradually refresh their cached answers.
  4. More and more networks begin showing the new value.

How to verify DNS changes

The fastest way is to run a DNS lookup and compare the published record with what you expect. Check the exact record type you changed:

  • A / AAAA for website IP addresses
  • CNAME for aliases like www
  • MX for email delivery
  • TXT for verification and email security
  • NS to confirm which DNS provider is authoritative
Good habit: If a DNS change does not appear, first confirm you changed the record in the correct DNS provider. If the NS records point elsewhere, you may be editing DNS in the wrong place.

How long does DNS propagation take?

There is no single universal timing because it depends on TTL values, resolver behavior, and which record type changed. Some updates appear in minutes, while others may take longer before all caches refresh.

Ways to reduce propagation pain

  • Lower the TTL before a planned migration, if possible
  • Double-check record type, hostname, and value before saving
  • Verify with a DNS lookup after the change
  • Be patient if some networks still show older cached results
Is DNS propagation really “global” at the same time?

No. Different resolvers refresh at different times, so some users may see the new record before others.

Does propagation mean my DNS provider is broken?

Usually not. In many cases the provider already published the new record, but cached answers elsewhere have not expired yet.

How do I know whether I changed the right DNS zone?

Check the domain’s NS records. They tell you which provider is authoritative for that domain’s DNS.

What record types are most affected by propagation?

Any DNS record can be affected by caching, including A, AAAA, CNAME, MX, and TXT records.
