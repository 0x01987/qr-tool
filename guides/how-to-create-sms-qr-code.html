Quick Answer

An SMS QR code opens the user’s messaging app with a phone number and optional prefilled message. It is useful when you want people to contact you quickly without typing, especially for quotes, support, reservations, and service requests. The easiest approach is to use a focused tool, keep the destination accurate, test the result on multiple devices, and add a clear call to action so the user knows exactly what happens after scanning or clicking.

Best use cases

  • Request a quote
  • Text for support
  • Text to reserve
  • Text to join waitlist
  • Text for pickup instructions

How to make it

  1. Enter the destination phone number.
  2. Optionally add a short prefilled message.
  3. Generate the QR code.
  4. Test the flow on different devices.

Keep the message short

Short prefilled text works best. Good examples include I’d like a quote, I need support, or I’m here for pickup. Long text can feel awkward or be truncated on some devices.

Things to watch

Some phones handle SMS links a little differently, so testing matters. Keep instructions clear and always verify the phone number is correct before publishing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I prefill the text message?
Yes. Most SMS QR tools let you prefill a short message along with the number.
Is SMS better than a contact form?
For quick responses and mobile-first audiences, it often is. It reduces friction because the user stays in their messaging app.
Can I use it on signage?
Yes. It works well on signs, flyers, counters, menus, and service vehicles.
